The starting point is simple: payment design shapes supply.

In supported or exempt accommodation, providers can often claim higher Housing Benefit than in ordinary private renting. That does not mean residents are fake. Residents are real people with real housing and support needs. The problem is that the payment rules can reward rapid growth in a claimable category, even when quality control and outcomes tracking are weak.

This is what people mean by a "cobra effect": policy rewards a measurable proxy, and the system expands the proxy instead of solving the underlying problem.

## Why This Is About Incentives, Not Denial of Need

There is genuine need in this system. Birmingham's own strategy includes substantial estimated need across multiple groups, with total local supported housing need estimated at 8,505 to 10,455 units (SH-001, p.17-18).

At the same time, Birmingham reported circa 21,317 transitional supported housing units in scope as of June 2021 (SH-001, p.5-6). That is far above its own estimated local range.

So the argument is not "need is fake." The argument is:
- need is real,
- money is attached to a category,
- and the category can scale faster than measured local need.

When those three conditions exist together, market actors do what market actors usually do: expand the claimable supply.

## The Hanoi Parallel (1902)

The more grounded historical analogy is the 1902 Hanoi rat-tail bounty, not the disputed Delhi cobra story. French colonial authorities paid for rat tails during a public-health campaign. The problem was real. The failure was incentive design: paying for tails rewarded tail production, not a rat-free city.

The supported-housing parallel is not about calling people "bounty objects." It is narrower: if funding rules reward the existence and occupation of a claimable unit, systems can optimize for unit throughput and occupancy before they optimize for long-term outcomes.

## What Is Proven in Birmingham

### 1) Large stock growth versus local-need estimates
- Circa 21,317 units in scope by June 2021 (SH-001, p.5-6).
- Local need estimate 8,505 to 10,455 (SH-001, p.17-18).
- Strategy text says the sector had "almost doubl[ed] in size in the previous 3 years" (SH-001, p.5).

That gap does not prove wrongdoing. It does prove that "local need alone" is not enough to explain observed stock levels.

### 2) Non-commissioned dominance and weak local control
- SH-001 estimates circa 20,000 units, 94% of the sector, as non-commissioned (SH-001, p.9).
- It also states the council has no influence over "myriad ... untracked referral routes" (SH-001, p.12).
- Interim management tables repeatedly note providers are "free to source own referrals" (SH-001, p.26-27).

This is an important structural point: if the local authority does not control most entry routes, local planning and housing strategy are operating downstream of external referral decisions.

### 3) Out-of-area referral evidence exists
The University of Birmingham / Spring Housing study identified 129 referral points and found 46 outside Birmingham (SH-004, p.25). It also notes no interviewed providers had a local-connection policy limiting access to people connected to Birmingham (SH-004, p.26).

The same report includes interview evidence that out-of-area prisons often treated Birmingham as "the only option" (SH-004, p.27).

Important caution: 46/129 is referral-point geography, not a full resident-origin census. It is still strong evidence that cross-boundary routing was part of the system.

### 4) National fiscal context supports "routing to cheaper pathways"
At national level, local authorities face severe pressure in temporary accommodation:
- subsidy loss of £204.5m in 2022-23 (SH-005, p.11),
- subsidy basis tied to January 2011 LHA rates (SH-005, p.11).

Those numbers explain why local systems are under pressure to move people away from expensive pathways. They do not prove any single local pathway is optimal; they explain why referral and placement behavior can be highly cost-sensitive.

## The LHA Cap and the 6.51m² Cobra Effect

The "cobra effect" is not limited to high-support exempt accommodation. It also drives the standard private HMO market where rent is capped by the **Local Housing Allowance (LHA)**.

When a payment cap is fixed (like LHA), a landlord cannot increase revenue by raising the rent per room. Instead, they increase revenue by **multiplying the number of rooms**. 

- **The Goal:** Maximize yield from a single property.
- **The Proxy:** Total room count.
- **The Result:** Conversion of family homes into 6-7 bedroom "tiny-room" HMOs, often built to the bare legal minimum of **6.51m²**.

In this scenario, the benefit system’s attempt to "cap" costs (LHA) actually incentivizes the production of the lowest-quality, most cramped housing possible, because that is the only way for the property owner to hit their yield targets.

## Two Sides of the Same Coin

Whether the goal is to **bypass** the LHA cap (via supported/exempt status) or to **maximize yield under** the LHA cap (via the 6.51m² loophole), the underlying dynamic is identical. The "Cobra Effect" occurs because the payment design rewards a proxy—occupancy in a claimable unit or room—rather than a social outcome.

- **The Supported Cobra:** Rewards the *classification* of a resident as "vulnerable" to unlock higher funding, leading to over-classification and weak support delivery.
- **The Private Cobra:** Rewards the *multiplication* of rooms to capture more LHA cheques, leading to the systematic conversion of family homes into "tiny-room" warehouses.

In both cases, capital flows toward the category with the most reliable, publicly-backed rent stream, and supply adapts to the specific shape of the subsidy, not the needs of the residents or the community.

## What Still Needs Better Data

The current public evidence is enough to show a structural incentive problem, but not enough to settle every contested claim.

### 1) Resident origin at person level
We still need robust borough-level fields for:
- prior postcode / prior local authority,
- local-connection status,
- referral source at person level.

Without those, we cannot responsibly quantify how much occupancy came from each origin pathway.

### 2) Migration share within supported-exempt occupancy
National homelessness and asylum-move-on trends are relevant context, but they do not directly quantify Birmingham's supported-exempt resident mix. Claims of migration dominance need direct linked data, not inference.

### 3) Support intensity versus classification
We need structured data on assessed support need, support hours delivered, and outcomes. Without this, debates collapse into anecdotes about "minimal support" versus "high need" cases.

### 4) Move-on outcomes and duration
Length of stay, exits to settled housing, repeat homelessness, and churn rates are central. If people cannot move on, even well-intended placements can become a warehousing mechanism.

## A More Precise Claim

The strongest evidence-backed claim today is:

Birmingham's supported-exempt market scaled far beyond local need estimates in a system where most supply was non-commissioned, referral routes were weakly controlled, and out-of-area referral pathways were clearly present. In that environment, payment design likely rewarded growth in claimable units faster than governance could track quality and outcomes.

That claim is serious, but bounded. It does not require claims about conspiracy, corruption, or any single driver. It follows from published counts, referral-route evidence, and documented institutional control gaps.

## Policy Test That Matters

If policymakers want to know whether this is a cobra-effect market, the test is operational:
- Tie funding and provider status to outcome data, not just occupancy and category eligibility.
- Require auditable referral-origin and local-connection reporting.
- Publish support-delivery and move-on metrics in a standard format.
- Align local authority powers with the scale of non-commissioned provision.

Until those controls exist, the system will keep rewarding what it can easily count: occupied claimable units.
