If you’ve read today's [BBC article](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/c4g4l55kyjeo) (“Ilfracombe High Street ‘no go area as drug incidents surge’”), the picture is grim but familiar to anyone watching coastal towns. 

Devon and Cornwall Police recorded **364 violent and sexual offences**, **287 anti-social behaviour incidents**, and **121 shoplifting cases** in central and east Ilfracombe in the year to March 2026. Locals describe people “undressed and screaming in the middle of the road” under the influence, shop workers being assaulted, and a high street that some residents now avoid entirely.

North Devon Council links the surge to poverty, poor housing, and health inequality. But the key line in the BBC piece reveals the structural engine:

> “Many former guest houses had been turned into flats housing people with ‘complex drug, alcohol or mental health needs’, while support services had been cut back.”

This isn’t just “local decline.” It’s the direct result of a national system that funnels high-needs people from expensive urban areas into cheap coastal housing stock—and it exposes a widespread myth about council housing duties.

### The Myth: \"Councils must house everyone locally\"
You’ll hear it repeated constantly: “Councils have to house everyone who turns up.” That’s not true. The **Housing Act 1996** creates a **temporary and limited** duty, not an unlimited local obligation.

- Councils must investigate and provide **temporary/emergency** accommodation while they assess a homeless application.
- If the person qualifies for the main housing duty, the council must “secure suitable accommodation”—but **only so far as reasonably practicable in their own district** (Section 208).
- In high-cost cities (London boroughs, Birmingham, Manchester, etc.), it is often **not** reasonably practicable to keep people in expensive local stock. So they look for a "relief valve."

### How the cross-council flow actually works
Here’s the step-by-step reality that explains Ilfracombe’s former guesthouses:

1. **The Referral:** An expensive urban borough (**Council A**) has a person with complex needs.
2. **The Opportunity:** Council A finds a **private supported housing provider** in a cheap coastal town like Ilfracombe with empty ex-guesthouses.
3. **The Discharge:** They offer the person a room there as the way to discharge their housing duty. If the person refuses, they risk being deemed “intentionally homeless.”
4. **The Payment:** Once the tenancy is signed, the person claims **Housing Benefit**. In “exempt/supported accommodation,” the rent is **not capped** by the Local Housing Allowance (LHA), so landlords can charge much higher rates to cover "support" that is often minimal or non-existent.
5. **The Shift:** Council A is now off the hook. The DWP (central government) pays the rent. The receiving town (**Ilfracombe**) pays the price in policing, NHS strain, and community impact.

Ilfracombe Town Council’s own **Strategic Plan 2024–2034** (Objective 10, p.30) states it plainly:

> “Supported living providers provide housing for vulnerable adults, **the majority of whom are from out of the area**. ITC believes that such providers should primarily be supporting Ilfracombe’s vulnerable adults and not those of other towns and cities."

### Why this system persists
- **Arbitrage:** Shipping people to cheap coastal stock ticks a statutory box for Council A at a lower cost than local hotels.
- **Yield-Chasing:** For a landlord, an empty seaside guest house is a liability. An "exempt accommodation" unit with guaranteed DWP rent is a high-yield asset.
- **Deinstitutionalisation without Funding:** People were moved into “community” housing, but funding for on-site support was cut. We are seeing the result on the high street.

### The Planistan Perspective
This is why transparency tools matter. Just as we track HMO planning applications in Greenwich, we need to map the **lineage of placements**. 

Ilfracombe isn’t failing by accident; it is the recipient of a national "dumping" policy that exploits cheap property and the lack of cross-borough data visibility. Until the incentives change—tighter rules on exempt rents and stronger local-connection enforcement—towns like Ilfracombe will continue to be the "relief valves" for a broken national housing market.
