**Ilfracombe High Street: How Out-of-Area Placements and the Housing Benefit System Turned a Seaside Town into a Dumping Ground**

Posted 13 May 2026

If you’ve read the BBC article published today (“Ilfracombe High Street ‘no go area as drug incidents surge’”), the picture is grim but familiar to anyone watching coastal towns. Devon and Cornwall Police recorded **364 violent and sexual offences**, **287 anti-social behaviour incidents**, and **121 shoplifting cases** in central and east Ilfracombe in the year to March 2026. Locals describe people “undressed and screaming in the middle of the road” under the influence, shop workers being assaulted, and a high street that some now avoid entirely.

Ilfracombe Town Council has written to the Police and Crime Commissioner calling the situation “intolerable” and demanding more officers. North Devon Council links the surge to poverty, poor housing, and health inequality (life expectancy in parts of the town is 14 years lower than in wealthier nearby areas). The key line in the BBC piece:

> “Many former guest houses had been turned into flats housing people with ‘complex drug, alcohol or mental health needs’, while support services had been cut back.”

This isn’t just “local decline.” It’s the direct result of a national system that funnels high-needs people from expensive urban areas into cheap coastal housing stock — and it exposes a widespread myth about council housing duties.

### The myth we’re all told
You’ll hear it repeated constantly: “Councils have to house everyone who turns up.” That’s not true. The **Housing Act 1996** creates a **temporary and limited** duty, not an unlimited local obligation.

- Councils must investigate and provide **temporary/emergency** accommodation while they assess a homeless application.
- If the person qualifies for the main housing duty, the council must “secure suitable accommodation” — but **only so far as reasonably practicable in their own district** (Section 208).
- In high-cost cities (London boroughs, Birmingham, Manchester, etc.), it is **not** reasonably practicable to keep people in expensive local stock forever. So they look elsewhere.

### How the cross-council flow actually works
Here’s the step-by-step reality that explains Ilfracombe’s former guesthouses:

1. **Person with complex needs** (addict, severe mental health issues, ex-prisoner, chaotic benefit claimant) presents as homeless to **Council A** (an expensive urban borough).
2. Council A houses them temporarily (hostel, B&B, etc.) while it tries to resolve the case.
3. Council A (or its partners — probation, mental health teams, housing associations) finds a **private supported housing provider or landlord** in a cheap coastal town like Ilfracombe with empty ex-guesthouses.
4. They offer the person a room/flat there as the way to discharge their duty. The person is told: this is the offer on the table — take it or you’re on the street.
5. The person accepts (temporary accommodation in Council A is often grim and short-term; refusing risks being deemed “intentionally homeless”).
6. Once the tenancy is signed, the person claims **Housing Benefit or the housing element of Universal Credit**. This is paid **nationally by the DWP** (central taxes) directly to the landlord. In “exempt/supported accommodation” the rent is **not capped** like normal private rents, so landlords can charge higher rates.
7. Council A is largely off the hook. They notify Council B (North Devon), but they don’t keep paying the rent. Council B processes the claim and deals with the knock-on effects (crime, ASB, NHS strain) with no extra funding.

Ilfracombe Town Council’s own **Strategic Plan 2024–2034** (Objective 10, p.30) states it plainly:

> “Supported living providers provide housing for vulnerable adults, **the majority of whom are from out of the area**. ITC believes that such providers should primarily be supporting Ilfracombe’s vulnerable adults and not those of other towns and cities. It will therefore seek to engage with other councils and regional authorities to dissuade them from continuing to direct such individuals to Ilfracombe, which now has a disproportionate number of residents in receipt of incapacity benefit as a result of such policies.”

They also note the legacy of Victorian buildings + collapsed B&B trade has created a perfect storm of HMOs, many now targeting benefit claimants from elsewhere.

### Why this system exists
- **Council A saves money**: Local rents are eye-watering; shipping people to cheap coastal stock ticks their statutory box cheaply.
- **Landlords profit**: Empty guesthouses + guaranteed DWP rent payments = reliable income.
- **National welfare design**: Housing benefit follows the person wherever they are placed. There is no strong mechanism stopping the export of problems.
- **Support cuts**: Deinstitutionalisation moved people into “community” housing, then funding for actual on-site support was squeezed. Result: visible street chaos.

This is the same perverse incentive machine we see in Blackpool, Hastings, Rhyl, and other deprived seaside towns. Ilfracombe isn’t uniquely failing — it’s receiving the overflow that expensive urban areas can’t (or won’t) house locally.

### What this means for understanding the bigger picture
The BBC article correctly identifies the housing conversions and service cuts, but the root cause is national policy + welfare rules, not just “local poverty.” Councils do **not** have to house everyone forever in their own expensive patch. The law gives them an escape hatch that coastal towns end up paying for.

North Devon Council is now investing in 16 affordable homes and a £3m “healthy homes” project, which is welcome. Ilfracombe Town Council is pushing back on the out-of-area pipeline. But until the incentives change — tighter rules on exempt accommodation rents, stronger local-connection enforcement, or actual funding that follows the person — towns like Ilfracombe will keep getting the concentrated chaos.

This is why transparency tools matter. Just as we track HMO planning applications locally, we need to track where supported living placements are coming from and who is really paying (spoiler: it’s national taxpayers, not the sending councils).

If you’re in Ilfracombe or seeing similar patterns elsewhere, the council’s own strategic plan and the latest police stats are public. The system isn’t broken by accident — it’s working exactly as the incentives dictate. Understanding the actual legal limits (instead of the myth that “councils must house everyone”) is the first step to fixing it.
