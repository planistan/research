Draft notes for later editing. This is deliberately over-complete. The aim is to preserve the research problem, the working hypothesis, the vocabulary, the data sources, the uncertainty, and the build plan for a possible dashboard.

## The actual problem

The financial incentive is not hard to see. If a house can be converted into rooms and those rooms can be treated as supported or exempt accommodation, the rent stream can be much higher and more publicly underwritten than an ordinary shared house. That part is not subtle.

The hard question is different:

> What changed over time in the real population and real housing stock such that many more people could be placed into this category?

The dashboard problem is hard because it is not enough to count planning applications, HMO licences, supported housing providers, or Housing Benefit claims. Those are only visible shadows of a flow system.

We need to describe the change in the mismatch between:

- people needing some form of housing, support, or crisis accommodation;
- homes or rooms available in social housing, private rented housing, ordinary HMOs, temporary accommodation, and supported housing;
- public payment categories that make some rooms more profitable than others;
- institutions with people to discharge, place, refer, or move on;
- local authorities with legal duties and budgets under pressure;
- private providers and property owners responding to the revenue structure.

The crude story is: a public payment category creates a high-yield room. Capital and operators produce those rooms. But the rooms only pay if real humans occupy them. So the more important story is about how the system finds, routes, retains, imports, reclassifies, or fails to move on real people.

This is why a dashboard is not just a charting problem. It is a lineage problem.

## Brutal working hypothesis

The working hypothesis is:

1. There is real need.
2. There is a real shortage of ordinary exits from crisis, homelessness, institutional discharge, and low-income single adult housing need.
3. The supported/exempt accommodation funding route makes a particular type of shared accommodation financially attractive.
4. Once that category exists, it becomes a pressure valve for many other broken systems.
5. The resulting growth is not explained by one group alone.
6. It is probably explained by a combination of blocked exits, stock conversion, weak regulation, local authority budget pressure, and inflows from multiple vulnerable populations.

The important correction is that "demand" here is not the same as normal consumer demand. Many residents are not choosing between ordinary rental products. They are being referred, discharged, placed, released, moved on, or left with no realistic alternative.

So "who are the extra people?" becomes a set of more precise questions:

- Are there more people in the relevant vulnerable categories?
- Are people staying longer because exits are blocked?
- Are people being moved into a borough from elsewhere because that borough has the rooms?
- Are ordinary housing problems being converted into supported housing claims because that is where the funding is?
- Are rooms being reclassified from ordinary HMO/private rented stock into supported/exempt stock?
- Are institutions using supported/exempt accommodation because their own discharge or move-on routes are exhausted?
- Are providers filling rooms from wider catchments because the local referral pool is not enough or because outside institutions are easier to work with?

That is the hard dashboard.

## The categories we need to keep separate

The vocabulary matters because otherwise everything gets called "supported housing" and the analysis collapses.

### HMO residents

Not all HMOs are supported housing. This needs to be said much more strongly.

An HMO is a property form: unrelated people sharing a house or flat, usually with some shared facilities. Supported housing is a support/funding/needs category. Exempt accommodation is a Housing Benefit category. These can overlap, but they are not the same thing.

This means HMO growth can be driven by ordinary private-rented-market pressure even when there is no supported housing angle at all. HMO residents can include:

- students;
- young professionals;
- key workers;
- migrant workers;
- low-income single adults priced out of self-contained flats;
- recently separated adults;
- people moving to a city for work;
- people who want flexible bills-included accommodation;
- people in informal or hidden bedsit arrangements;
- families or couples squeezed into rooms in the worst end of the market;
- temporary accommodation households;
- supported/exempt accommodation residents.

This is why the phrase "HMO demand" is dangerous. It can mean ordinary shared living, student demand, low-income rental stress, illegal overcrowding, temporary accommodation spillover, or supported/exempt accommodation. A dashboard that treats every HMO application as a supported housing signal will overstate the supported housing story and miss the wider private-rented-sector story.

Industry commentary also points to ordinary HMO market dynamics separate from supported housing. Ocasa/HMO Magazine material from late 2022 discussed a sharp fall in available rental homes across Britain, which matters because general rental scarcity pushes people toward cheaper shared rooms. Octane Capital analysis reported in Mortgage Solutions put England's HMO stock at 489,701 in 2021/2022, down about 21,000 properties over two years, while also noting regional variation: the West Midlands and Yorkshire and the Humber grew even as England overall fell. CBRE has separately framed HMOs as a major student accommodation pressure valve, reporting that more than 35% of students rely on HMOs in cities such as Manchester, Nottingham and Edinburgh. The Guardian's "Bedsit Britain" reporting points to a darker part of the same broad market: unlicensed and overcrowded large HMOs, with tenants drawn by cheaper rents amid the cost-of-living crisis.

So the broader HMO market is not just a mask for supported housing. Supported/exempt HMOs are one high-risk, high-incentive subset of a much larger shared-housing system.

### Social housing demanders

People or households seeking ordinary settled social housing. This is a demand for tenure and affordability. The need may include vulnerability, but the housing product is not necessarily a care/support product.

Social housing demand is visible through waiting lists, homelessness acceptances, allocation schemes, new social lettings, transfers, and related local authority housing data. It is not the same as supported housing demand.

### Supported housing demanders

People needing accommodation with some level of care, support, or supervision. This includes very different groups: older people, learning disability, mental health, homelessness, rough sleeping, domestic abuse, substance misuse, care leavers, ex-offenders, people leaving hospital, and people with multiple needs.

Supported housing is partly a housing category and partly a support category. It can be long-term or short-term. It can be commissioned by public bodies or non-commissioned. It can be good-quality specialist provision or weak shared accommodation with minimal support.

### Specified accommodation / exempt accommodation claimants

This is a Housing Benefit classification. The Government's Supported Housing Review 2023 describes specified accommodation as a four-way classification for Housing Benefit assessment: exempt accommodation, managed properties, refuges, and local authority hostels. Exempt accommodation is the key category because it can be exempt from ordinary Local Housing Allowance caps.

This is not identical to "supported housing" in the everyday sense. It is a payment and eligibility category.

### Temporary accommodation demanders

Households owed homelessness duties who are placed in temporary accommodation because settled housing is not available. Temporary accommodation includes nightly paid accommodation, hotels, B&Bs, private sector leased accommodation, hostels, and other arrangements.

Temporary accommodation is not the same thing as supported housing. But the two systems can interact. If temporary accommodation is expensive for the council and supported/exempt accommodation is more recoverable through Housing Benefit, there is an incentive to use supported/exempt rooms as an alternative pressure valve, especially for single adults and people with complex needs.

### Housing slots

The dashboard needs slots as well as people:

- ordinary social housing slots;
- new social housing lets;
- temporary accommodation slots;
- ordinary private rented rooms;
- ordinary HMO rooms;
- supported housing units;
- specified accommodation Housing Benefit claims;
- exempt accommodation rooms;
- hotels/B&B/nightly-paid temporary accommodation rooms;
- institutional discharge slots, such as prison release, hospital discharge, or care leaver accommodation pathways.

The central dynamic is not just population growth. It is the movement of people between categories and the movement of housing stock between categories.

## Social housing and supported housing are different

One confusion worth making explicit:

- Social housing is mainly about ownership/tenure/rent level and allocation.
- Supported housing is about accommodation plus care, support, or supervision.

They can overlap. A registered provider can provide supported housing that is also social housing. But they are not the same concept.

This matters because supported housing can become a substitute holding pen when ordinary social housing is unavailable. A person may not need "support" in the deep clinical sense forever. They may need somewhere cheap, stable, and not dangerous. But if ordinary social housing is blocked, and the only available public-funded room is a supported/exempt room, they may become a supported housing resident in practice.

This is a key analytical risk. A rise in supported housing occupancy may mean:

- real growth in support needs;
- better recognition of support needs;
- social housing scarcity pushing people into supported categories;
- private rented scarcity pushing people into supported categories;
- temporary accommodation cost pressure pushing councils toward alternatives;
- providers reclassifying rooms into higher-revenue categories;
- institutions using supported accommodation because they have nowhere else to send people.

Those are very different explanations. A dashboard that cannot separate them may look authoritative while actually being mostly decorative.

There is an equivalent risk on the HMO side. A rise in HMO planning applications may mean:

- more student demand;
- more young professional shared living;
- affordability pressure in the ordinary private rented sector;
- family homes being carved into rooms because per-room rent is higher;
- landlords shifting from whole-house lets to room-by-room lets;
- temporary accommodation providers moving into the private HMO market;
- supported/exempt accommodation growth;
- unlicensed or hidden overcrowded bedsit growth becoming partially visible through enforcement.

Those are not the same story. The supported/exempt story is important because the public payment incentive can be unusually strong, but it should not swallow the entire HMO analysis.

## Birmingham is not just a scandal story. It is a model of the mechanism.

Birmingham is useful because the evidence is unusually explicit.

The University of Birmingham / Spring Housing report on shared exempt accommodation in Birmingham described a largely hidden and poorly documented sector. It found that non-commissioned exempt accommodation was often direct-access, transitional, concentrated in shared HMOs, and occupied by a wide cross-section of vulnerable people with multiple or complex needs. It also noted that the model sat in a wider policy environment of constrained access to social housing and private rented accommodation.

The same report is particularly important on referral geography. During the study, researchers identified 129 separate access or referral routes into exempt accommodation. Criminal-justice organisations and local authority departments were the two largest referral clusters, and in both cases many referring organisations were outside Birmingham. The report says that, excluding internet property sites and word of mouth, 46 of the 129 referral points identified were outside Birmingham. It also describes direct referrals from fourteen prisons, several outside the West Midlands and Staffordshire prison estate.

This is what "out-of-area dumping" means in plain English:

> institutions outside Birmingham had people they needed to house or discharge, Birmingham had a developed market of rooms, and people were sent or referred into Birmingham because it was available.

That is not necessarily a single conspiracy. It can emerge from every actor doing the obvious thing locally:

- a prison needs a release address;
- a homelessness team needs a bed;
- a hospital needs discharge;
- a care leaver team needs accommodation;
- a provider needs occupancy;
- a landlord needs rent;
- a council wants to avoid more expensive crisis accommodation;
- a benefits system pays the eligible rent if the category is satisfied.

The result can still be a human dumping mechanism. If other boroughs, prisons, hospitals, or teams send people into a city because that is where the rooms are, the city becomes the sink for other systems' unresolved housing demand.

The Birmingham report also records providers taking referrals from inside and outside the city, and notes that none of the accommodation providers interviewed had a local connection policy restricting access only to people with a Birmingham connection. The report includes a criminal justice practitioner's explanation that people were being released into Birmingham because Birmingham had the housing stock and had developed the market. That is the mechanism in one sentence.

Birmingham City Council's own supported housing strategy draft described the system as broken. It said the council had no influence over entry, expansion, exit, referral routes, type or intensity of support required, numbers, location, or density of provision. It also said provision was exempt from licensing, planning permission, and local rent limits, and that the council faced extreme challenges scrutinising more than 20,000 units. Its pilot inspections found thousands of category 1 and 2 hazards and evidence of poor support, poor training, anti-social behaviour issues, and even criminality in parts of the sector.

This is why Birmingham matters for the national dashboard. It gives us a list of fields we wish every area had:

- resident previous location;
- referral agency;
- referral agency location;
- local connection status;
- provider;
- landlord;
- registered provider or managing agent chain;
- property address;
- accommodation type;
- support type;
- commissioning status;
- Housing Benefit category;
- rent and service charge;
- duration of stay;
- exit destination;
- repeat homelessness or repeat placement.

Most of those fields are not available in a clean public dataset.

## The cobra effect and the Hanoi rat-tail problem

The incentive problem resembles the "cobra effect" story, but the best-documented version is probably the Hanoi rat-tail bounty rather than the popular Delhi cobra story. The Delhi cobra story is widely repeated but historically shaky. The Hanoi case is better documented: the colonial authority paid a bounty for rat tails, and people could respond by producing tails rather than reducing rats.

The useful point is not that the rooms or residents are fake. The cobras and rats were real too, at least in the structure of the lesson. The policy paid for a measurable output, and actors learned how to produce the paid-for output.

For supported/exempt accommodation:

- the state pays for a category of room;
- the market learns which paperwork, provider structure, and support threshold unlocks the payment;
- capital learns the rent stream is financeable;
- referral agencies learn which providers will take hard-to-place people;
- providers learn how to maintain occupancy;
- weak exits mean residents churn around the category rather than moving on.

The institutional memory is not some mystical cultural inheritance. It is practical market memory:

- landlords remember which property layouts produce higher rent;
- consultants remember how to package the model;
- managing agents remember which registered providers can be used;
- providers remember the minimum support story needed to sustain claims;
- councils remember which placement routes reduce immediate pressure;
- prisons, hospitals, and homelessness teams remember where beds are available;
- investors remember that Housing Benefit can underwrite the cash flow.

This is why a narrowly moral story is too weak. The system teaches everyone what pays. Once learned, the model persists.

## Why "more domestic abuse" or "low TFR" is not enough

The naive demand explanation would say: there are more people who need support, so more supported housing appears.

Some of that is true. But it is too broad.

Domestic abuse, mental health need, substance misuse, prison release, care leaving, rough sleeping, asylum and refugee routes, and older people's support needs all matter. But the dashboard cannot just use one of them as a magic explanatory variable.

Low fertility means natural population growth is not the obvious explanation for a sudden local increase in adult single-person housing need. But the relevant population is not "all residents". It is the pool of people who:

- cannot access ordinary private rented housing;
- cannot access social housing quickly;
- are single or treated as needing shared accommodation;
- have some vulnerability or support marker;
- are being discharged from an institution;
- are owed homelessness prevention or relief help;
- are leaving asylum, care, prison, hospital, family breakdown, or unsafe accommodation;
- can be placed into a Housing Benefit-supported accommodation category.

The question is therefore not "did Birmingham's total population rise enough?" The question is:

> Did the stock of people in the crisis/single-adult/vulnerable/no-exit pool rise, and did the duration of time spent in that pool rise?

Even if entries were stable, blocked exits could increase the stock. If people used to spend three months in a pathway and now spend eighteen months, the number of occupied rooms can rise sharply without a proportionate rise in new people entering each month.

This is one reason the problem is hard. We need flows and durations, not just annual counts.

## A useful flow model

The dashboard should model people and rooms as stocks and flows.

### Person states

Possible person states:

- rough sleeping;
- hidden homelessness / sofa surfing;
- domestic abuse refuge need;
- prison custody;
- prison release / probation accommodation need;
- hospital discharge accommodation need;
- care leaver accommodation need;
- asylum support accommodation;
- refugee move-on / homelessness presentation after grant of status;
- homelessness prevention case;
- homelessness relief case;
- temporary accommodation;
- ordinary private rented room;
- ordinary HMO room;
- supported housing;
- exempt accommodation;
- commissioned supported housing;
- non-commissioned supported housing;
- social housing waiting list;
- new social housing let;
- settled private rented housing;
- unknown / lost to follow-up.

### Housing states

Possible property or room states:

- owner-occupied dwelling;
- ordinary private rented dwelling;
- ordinary HMO;
- licensed HMO;
- unlicensed HMO;
- social rented dwelling;
- supported social housing unit;
- commissioned supported housing unit;
- non-commissioned supported/exempt unit;
- temporary accommodation unit;
- hotel/B&B/nightly paid TA;
- empty/void;
- converted, demolished, sold, or removed from social stock.

### Transitions

The meaningful transitions include:

- private rented home -> HMO;
- HMO -> supported/exempt HMO;
- social rented home -> sale under Right to Buy;
- social rented home -> demolition or loss;
- new affordable/social build -> social stock;
- homelessness presentation -> prevention/relief;
- prevention/relief -> temporary accommodation;
- temporary accommodation -> supported/exempt accommodation;
- prison/hospital/care/asylum route -> supported/exempt accommodation;
- supported/exempt accommodation -> settled housing;
- supported/exempt accommodation -> another supported/exempt provider;
- supported/exempt accommodation -> homelessness again;
- local resident -> out-of-area placement;
- out-of-area person -> local supported/exempt placement.

This is why the single-borough view is both necessary and misleading. A borough can be the location of rooms, the administering authority for Housing Benefit, the originating authority of a homeless duty, the receiving authority for a placement, or none of those cleanly. The person, the property, the referring institution, the paying body, and the responsible authority may not line up.

## What data is available

There is useful data. It is not hopeless. But the data is fragmented and usually does not connect the person flow to the property flow.

### Supported housing / specified accommodation

The Government's Supported Housing Review 2023 is the best overview. It estimates 634,000 supported housing units and 215,770 specified accommodation claims recorded on the Housing Benefit system in November 2023. It also estimates unmet need between 179,600 and 388,100 additional units, and by 2040 a need for 361,700 to 640,700 additional units. It notes that 83% of specified accommodation claims were exempt accommodation.

The review is useful for national scale, definitions, funding, commissioning, and the size of the measurement problem. It is not enough for local resident origin or referral flow.

The National Audit Office investigation is useful because it states plainly that there are no good data on the numbers of units and people living in supported housing, and because it explains the fragmented oversight problem. DWP is responsible for Housing Benefit, DLUHC/MHCLG for supply and quality, DHSC for independent living policy, local authorities administer Housing Benefit and inspect properties, and other departments are involved for specific groups. Nobody owns the whole system.

Housing Benefit data is now mainly accessed through DWP Stat-Xplore. Historically, supported/specified accommodation flags have had under-recording and quality issues. DWP's background note says a new Housing Benefit accommodation type breakdown in Stat-Xplore was released in February 2026, allowing users to identify specified accommodation and temporary accommodation from September 2025 onward. That is important but it means long time series for this exact classification are still limited.

There are also FOI-based datasets, such as the London Assembly/DWP "Exempt housing in London" response. That document is useful precisely because its caveats are strong: it says the underlying Single Housing Benefit Extract reflects local authority data, that supported accommodation cases identified in SHBE do not align with published statistics, that under-reporting affects the specified/exempt classification, and that trend comparisons should be treated cautiously.

Conclusion: HB/Stat-Xplore can probably support a dashboard of claimant counts by area and accommodation type from September 2025 onward, and some historic approximations or FOI datasets. It is weak for long-run, consistent, local, property-level, resident-origin analysis.

### Homelessness and temporary accommodation

MHCLG statutory homelessness live tables are strong for homelessness applications, prevention, relief, main duty, temporary accommodation, and reasons for loss of last settled home. The data is now based on H-CLIC; older P1E-based tables are discontinued. The live tables include detailed local authority-level tables and flows tables.

This is probably one of the strongest pieces of the dashboard.

But it still has limits:

- it is a homelessness system dataset, not a supported housing resident dataset;
- it does not tell us every supported/exempt placement;
- out-of-area placement fields may not fully resolve actual resident origin, referral origin, and property location;
- the categories may not identify whether a person moved into non-commissioned exempt accommodation;
- it is household-level homelessness administration, not a full person lineage.

Still, this is the best starting point for the temporary accommodation and homelessness side.

### Social housing stock and lettings

Local Authority Housing Statistics (LAHS) gives local authority housing data, including stock, waiting lists, lettings, vacant stock, rents, and condition. The GOV.UK collection describes LAHS as an annual return by local authorities in England, covering a range of topics split into sections.

CORE social housing lettings is a flow dataset for new social housing lettings. The GOV.UK collection says CORE records characteristics of new social housing tenants and the homes they rent, including trends over time, tenant characteristics, and tenancy agreements. The technical notes explicitly distinguish CORE as a flow measure of new social housing lettings from LAHS as a stock measure.

Social housing sales and Right to Buy data is separately published in live tables. These tables cover sales of social housing stock, with separate Right to Buy tables.

Together these can support:

- social housing stock over time;
- waiting list pressure;
- annual new social lets;
- flow into settled social housing;
- Right to Buy/social stock losses;
- demolitions;
- possibly a measure of blocked move-on capacity.

The weakness is that these datasets do not directly say: "person X in supported/exempt accommodation could not move on because no social let was available." We have to infer pressure from stock, lets, waiting list, and duration.

### Immigration, asylum, refugee and resettlement routes

Home Office regional and local authority immigration-group tables provide local authority data for Homes for Ukraine arrivals, Afghan resettlement, and asylum seekers receiving support. The latest table is published as a regional/local authority dataset and includes local authority breakdowns.

This is necessary but insufficient.

It can help answer whether asylum support or resettlement populations changed in a place. It may also help identify pressure points created by asylum accommodation and refugee move-on. But it will not usually tell us whether someone later entered supported/exempt housing after a grant of status, or whether a particular supported housing resident entered via an asylum/refugee pathway.

The danger is using migration as a lazy explanation. Migration-related flows may be part of the story, but the dashboard needs to separate:

- asylum seekers still in Home Office support;
- people in asylum hotels;
- people with refugee status facing move-on/homelessness;
- Homes for Ukraine and Afghan resettlement groups;
- broader international migration;
- internal UK migration and institutional discharge.

Those are different populations with different housing routes.

### Criminal justice

MoJ offender management statistics cover prison and probation flows: receptions, discharges, probation starts and terminations, and caseloads. This can describe the scale of prison release and probation caseload pressure.

But the public statistics do not directly provide a clean local series of "people released from prison into supported/exempt accommodation in borough X by previous home authority and provider." Birmingham's qualitative evidence shows this is exactly the kind of field we need.

Criminal justice is likely important because prison release creates hard deadlines and high placement pressure. If a provider will accept people quickly and with low conditionality, the provider becomes part of the release infrastructure. But proving the flow quantitatively will probably require FOI requests, local partnership data, or provider/referral records.

### Care leavers, domestic abuse, health and mental health

These groups are essential but unevenly measured for this purpose.

Care leaver data exists through local authority returns and Department for Education statistics, but connecting care-leaver housing outcome to supported/exempt accommodation at property/provider level is hard.

Domestic abuse refuge provision and homelessness reason data can show some demand, but not necessarily supported/exempt placement outcomes.

Mental health, hospital discharge, learning disability, autism, substance misuse, and multiple needs are spread across NHS, local authority adult social care, public health, provider, and commissioned service datasets. The most important fields may not be public, may be sensitive, and may not align geographically.

### Planning, HMO licensing and property data

This is where Planistan can directly contribute.

Planning applications can reveal:

- HMO conversions;
- supported housing proposals;
- changes of use;
- applicant and agent networks;
- repeated proposal text;
- spatial clustering;
- timing around Article 4 directions or licensing changes.

HMO licensing data can reveal:

- licensed HMO locations;
- room counts;
- licence holders;
- managing agents;
- enforcement actions, where published.

Land Registry and company data can add:

- ownership;
- purchase date;
- sale price;
- company networks;
- refinances or charge data, where available.

But planning data is not a complete supported housing dataset, and HMO data is not automatically supported housing data. Some changes do not require planning permission. Some exempt accommodation may sit inside existing HMOs or ordinary houses. Some ordinary HMOs may have nothing to do with support, care, supervision, Housing Benefit specified accommodation, or homelessness duties. Some HMO data may not mark whether the property is supported/exempt. Some provider structures deliberately separate landlord, leaseholder, managing agent, registered provider, and support provider.

Planning data can generate risk flags and entity networks. It cannot by itself prove resident origin or support quality.

The dashboard therefore needs two separate classification axes:

- property form: single dwelling, small HMO, large HMO, bedsit, hostel, hotel/B&B, self-contained supported unit;
- resident/payment pathway: ordinary private rented, student, key worker/professional, low-income PRS, temporary accommodation, commissioned supported housing, non-commissioned supported/exempt accommodation, asylum/refugee route, criminal justice route, care/hospital route.

The hard cases are the overlaps. A large HMO used for ordinary students is not the same thing as a large HMO used for non-commissioned exempt accommodation. A poor-quality hidden bedsit may be exploitative without being "supported housing". A temporary accommodation HMO may create similar neighbourhood and welfare issues while sitting in a different funding pathway.

### Can the council tell what it will be used for?

Sometimes at application stage, but often not with confidence.

Councils can read the planning application, supporting statements, management plans, design/access statements, fire strategy, transport statement, equalities material, and officer report. If the applicant says "supported living", "temporary accommodation", "vulnerable adults", "ex-offenders", "care, support or supervision", "registered provider", "housing association", "social rent", "affordable rent", "asylum accommodation", or "move-on accommodation", the intended pathway may be visible.

But planning permission is not a full resident-lineage system. A proposal can be described simply as "conversion to HMO" or "change of use from C3 to C4/sui generis HMO" without saying whether the eventual rooms will be ordinary private rental, student rooms, temporary accommodation, supported/exempt accommodation, or something else. Even where the applicant's initial business model is disclosed, the actual use can change later if the new use does not require a fresh planning permission or if enforcement never sees the change.

HMO licensing is also limited. The licence can identify the licence holder, manager, maximum occupancy, and basic suitability/safety conditions. It does not normally classify the residents by pathway. A licensed HMO can be ordinary PRS, student housing, temporary accommodation, or supported/exempt accommodation.

Housing Benefit data is stronger for actual supported/exempt use, but only after occupation and claim administration. It is also not normally public at property level. Social housing evidence is stronger if the property is owned or managed by a local authority or registered provider, but registered-provider involvement alone does not prove the exact use of the specific property.

So the answer is:

- **Before approval:** councils may know if the applicant discloses it or if the documents make it obvious.
- **At approval:** councils can sometimes condition management plans, occupancy numbers, or use, but they cannot easily police every later resident pathway through planning alone.
- **After occupation:** the strongest evidence comes from licensing, Housing Benefit, commissioning, provider records, inspection/enforcement, complaints, and FOI.
- **For the public:** without FOI or published local registers, many properties will remain "unknown" or "probable" rather than proven.

This should become a data-quality flag in the dashboard: `intended_use_evidence`, `actual_use_evidence`, and `classification_confidence`.

### Which HMOs should people object to?

The sharper point is that residents should not object to every HMO as if all HMOs are the same.

The public risk profile is different for:

- a student HMO;
- a professional/key-worker flatshare;
- ordinary low-cost private rented rooms;
- a hidden overcrowded bedsit;
- a council temporary accommodation HMO;
- an asylum accommodation HMO;
- a probation/ex-offender referral HMO;
- a substance-misuse or complex-needs supported HMO;
- a non-commissioned exempt accommodation HMO with weak support.

Student housing and ordinary private accommodation can still produce legitimate objections: parking, noise, bins, loss of family housing, overcrowding, poor management, and pressure on local services. But those are not the same objections as the ones raised by supported/exempt or institutional referral accommodation.

For asylum accommodation, prison/probation-referred accommodation, substance-misuse accommodation, and complex-needs supported housing, the questions are more serious:

- Who is referring residents?
- Are residents local or imported from elsewhere?
- Is this a commissioned service or a private/non-commissioned provider filling rooms?
- What risk assessment is done?
- Are incompatible residents being mixed?
- Is there on-site staff or only light-touch floating support?
- Who is accountable if the placement fails?
- Is there a move-on route into settled housing?
- What is the provider's enforcement, complaint, safeguarding, or regulatory history?

This is not an argument that vulnerable people should not be housed. It is an argument that the public should know what pathway is being created. Objection should be proportionate to the actual use, risk, management model, and evidence. A blanket anti-HMO position will miss the important distinction between ordinary shared housing and publicly funded crisis/institutional accommodation.

### Can it become Airbnb?

Short-term letting is another possible property state and should not be confused with HMO/support use.

In London, residential property can generally be used for short-term letting for no more than 90 nights in a calendar year without planning permission, provided the legal conditions are met. More than that needs planning permission because it is treated as temporary sleeping accommodation and a material change of use. Outside London, the position depends more on ordinary material-change-of-use judgment and local restrictions, though national short-let reforms have been consulted on.

For a converted HMO, Airbnb-style use raises separate questions:

- Is the property still in lawful HMO use?
- Are rooms let individually for short stays, or is the whole property let?
- Does the use trigger a material change to temporary sleeping accommodation?
- Is there a London 90-night issue?
- Do licence conditions, planning conditions, lease terms, mortgage terms, insurance, fire safety duties, or management rules permit it?
- Is the property being used as a hostel/hotel in practice?

The data trail is different. Airbnb/short-let evidence may come from platform listings, screenshots, guest reviews, council complaints, planning enforcement, council tax/business rates signals, waste/noise records, or a future short-let register if available. HMO licensing alone will not usually tell us this.

For the model, add a property state:

- `short_term_let_or_temporary_sleeping_accommodation`

and a warning:

- a property can move from ordinary dwelling to HMO to supported/exempt HMO, but it can also move from ordinary dwelling or HMO to short-term-let use. Those are different financial incentives and different resident impacts.

## What data is missing

The missing fields are the important fields.

To answer the question properly, for each supported/exempt placement we would want:

- property address;
- room/unit count;
- accommodation category;
- Housing Benefit category;
- rent;
- service charge;
- support charge;
- provider;
- landlord;
- managing agent;
- registered provider, if any;
- commissioned or non-commissioned status;
- resident previous address or previous local authority;
- resident local connection status;
- referral source;
- referral source geography;
- statutory pathway, if any;
- prior state: rough sleeping, prison, hospital, care, asylum support, temporary accommodation, sofa surfing, private rented loss, domestic abuse, etc.;
- date entered;
- date exited;
- exit destination;
- reason for exit;
- whether the resident returned to homelessness or another supported/exempt placement;
- complaint/enforcement/safeguarding history;
- whether the placement met assessed support need.

For general HMO classification, we also need:

- intended use stated in planning documents;
- actual use observed after occupation;
- whether the HMO is ordinary PRS, student, professional/key-worker, temporary accommodation, asylum accommodation, probation/criminal justice referral, supported/exempt, social housing, or short-term let;
- evidence type for that label;
- confidence score;
- date of classification, because use can change.

The public datasets mostly give fragments:

- homelessness tables give homelessness system flows;
- LAHS gives social housing stock and waiting list data;
- CORE gives new social lettings;
- Right to Buy tables give some stock losses;
- Stat-Xplore gives HB claim counts, with improving but still limited accommodation type breakdowns;
- Home Office tables give some local immigration/asylum support populations;
- MoJ gives prison/probation flows;
- planning/licensing data gives property conversion and local supply signals;
- Birmingham-style reports give qualitative referral mechanisms.

The hard bit is joining them.

## Why a dashboard is hard

### 1. The person and the property are in different datasets

Housing Benefit knows claims. Planning knows applications. HMO licensing knows properties. Homelessness data knows applications and duties. CORE knows social lettings. Immigration data knows support populations. Prison data knows releases. None of them naturally share a stable public person-property-referral key.

### 2. Geography is ambiguous

The relevant geographies can differ:

- where the person previously lived;
- where the person has a local connection;
- which local authority owes a homelessness duty;
- where the property is physically located;
- which local authority administers Housing Benefit;
- where the referral agency is based;
- where the institution is based;
- where the provider is registered;
- where the landlord is registered.

If we only map property location, we may miss the import/export flow of people.

### 3. Categories changed and data quality changed

The supported/specified accommodation flags have had under-recording and classification problems. DWP only introduced a new accommodation-type breakdown in Stat-Xplore from September 2025 data, published in February 2026. This improves future monitoring but weakens long-run time series.

If a chart shows a jump, we must ask whether it is a real increase, a recording change, a provider reclassification, or a data-quality exercise.

### 4. "Need" is not one variable

There is no single "supported housing demander" dataset. Need is assembled from many routes: homelessness, prison release, care leaving, hospital discharge, domestic abuse, mental health, asylum/refugee move-on, rough sleeping, substance misuse, learning disability, older age, and blocked access to ordinary housing.

Each route has its own data owner, privacy constraints, definitions, and geography.

There is also no single "HMO demander" dataset. Ordinary HMO demand is assembled from student accommodation pressure, private rent inflation, deposit constraints, migration for work or study, low wages, relationship breakdown, people leaving family homes later, key-worker housing need, and the loss or unaffordability of self-contained rental stock. Supported/exempt demand is only one branch of that larger HMO demand tree.

### 5. Stock pressure is a duration problem

A shortage can worsen even if inflows are flat. If move-on slows down, the stock of people in supported housing or temporary accommodation rises. So the dashboard needs durations and exits, not just entries.

For example:

- entries per month may be stable;
- social housing lets may fall;
- private rents may rise;
- LHA may not cover rents;
- residents stay longer;
- rooms remain full;
- providers expand because occupancy risk is low.

Without duration, the dashboard will misread the mechanism.

### 6. The key evidence may require FOI

The Birmingham evidence we most want nationally is not a standard official dataset. It came from local research, stakeholder interviews, referral mapping, and local pilot work.

For a serious dashboard, we probably need FOI templates for:

- supported/exempt Housing Benefit claims by provider and month;
- specified accommodation claims by category;
- registered provider / managing agent / landlord records;
- provider lists and room counts;
- inspection outcomes;
- HHSRS hazards;
- complaints;
- referral source summaries;
- local connection checks;
- out-of-area placement counts;
- temporary accommodation placements by destination authority;
- prison/hospital/care-leaver referral counts, if held.

Many FOIs will be refused, partially answered, inconsistently answered, or provided in messy spreadsheets. That is still useful, but it means the dashboard has to track provenance and quality.

### 7. There are ethical and disclosure issues

The dashboard must avoid exposing vulnerable residents, addresses in a way that increases risk, or small-cell sensitive categories. Property-level mapping may be useful for planning and enforcement but can also create harm if it effectively identifies residents.

The public dashboard might need aggregation, suppression, or separate private/admin views.

## A realistic dashboard build path

### Phase 1: vocabulary and model

Define the core categories:

- social housing demander;
- supported housing demander;
- temporary accommodation demander;
- ordinary HMO resident;
- supported/exempt resident;
- institutional discharge resident;
- local resident;
- out-of-area resident;
- commissioned supported slot;
- non-commissioned supported/exempt slot;
- social housing slot;
- temporary accommodation slot.

Create a simple stock-flow model. The point is not mathematical perfection. It is to stop the analysis from collapsing into slogans.

### Phase 2: official baseline panel

Build a local authority by year/quarter panel from official sources:

- MHCLG homelessness live tables: applications, prevention, relief, main duty, temporary accommodation, reasons for homelessness, outcomes, out-of-area TA where available.
- LAHS: social housing stock, waiting lists, lettings, vacant stock.
- CORE: new social lettings and tenant characteristics.
- Social housing sales / Right to Buy: stock losses.
- DWP Stat-Xplore: Housing Benefit claims, specified accommodation and temporary accommodation breakdown from September 2025 onward.
- Home Office local immigration group tables: asylum support, Homes for Ukraine, Afghan schemes.
- MoJ offender management: prison/probation release and caseload indicators.
- Planistan planning data: HMO/supported housing applications, applicant/agent networks, proposal text, geographies.
- HMO licensing/open council data where available.

This phase can produce a rough dashboard, but it should be clearly labelled as a pressure dashboard, not a proof of resident origin.

It should also be clear whether each chart is measuring HMO pressure, supported/exempt pressure, temporary accommodation pressure, or general private-rented-sector pressure. These can correlate without being the same.

### Phase 3: Birmingham-style case study

Pick one or a few boroughs/cities and go deeper.

Birmingham is a known case, but London boroughs or another high-growth area may be better if the local planning data is stronger. The case study should attempt to reconstruct:

- property supply growth;
- provider networks;
- referral routes;
- local/out-of-area split;
- temporary accommodation pressure;
- social housing move-on pressure;
- enforcement/quality signals;
- Housing Benefit claim growth.

This is where FOI becomes central.

### Phase 4: FOI and scraper module

Create a small, separate module in the repo for housing-flow data collection, not mixed into the core planning UI prematurely.

Needed tools:

- source registry;
- downloader for official datasets;
- FOI request tracker;
- parser for spreadsheets/ODS files;
- schema for local authority panel;
- schema for provider/property records;
- provenance metadata;
- quality flags;
- dashboard export tables.

This should live as a parallel data pipeline that can later feed the main UI or a dedicated dashboard.

### Phase 5: dashboard

The first useful dashboard should not overclaim. It should show:

- social housing stock and lets over time;
- waiting list / homelessness pressure over time;
- temporary accommodation counts and out-of-area placements;
- specified/exempt accommodation claim counts where available;
- HMO planning application counts and geography;
- supported/exempt planning application counts where proposal text or use class evidence supports that label;
- ordinary HMO vs supported/exempt classification confidence;
- resident pathway risk flags where evidence exists: student, ordinary PRS, temporary accommodation, asylum accommodation, probation/criminal justice referral, substance-misuse/complex-needs support, social/registered-provider, short-term let;
- provider/applicant/agent networks;
- population and migration context;
- prison/probation/asylum/care-leaver indicators where available;
- data quality and missingness.

It also needs an operator accountability view. A bad HMO/support problem is often not visible from one property at a time. The dashboard should be able to show when the same owner, licence holder, managing agent, registered provider, support provider, applicant or agent appears repeatedly across boroughs, and whether that network is linked to hazards, licence breaches, enforcement, complaints, poor support, weak move-on, regulatory judgements or formal sanctions. That is the evidence base for an operator risk register or review queue.

This should not be a casual blacklist. The categories need to be evidential:

- automated risk signal;
- allegation or complaint;
- public council enforcement;
- regulator judgement;
- tribunal/court outcome;
- formal restriction or banning order;
- resolved/remediated.

The point is to help councils, residents, journalists and researchers identify repeat bad actors early enough that formal systems can refuse, condition, inspect, stop placing, stop commissioning, or pursue enforcement before the same network expands further.

It should make the hard question visible:

> Are rooms increasing because more people enter crisis, because exits are blocked, because people are imported from elsewhere, because properties are reclassified, or because all of these happen at once?

### Phase 6: causal caution

Even a good dashboard will probably not prove causality on its own.

It can narrow the possibilities:

- If supported/exempt claims rise while homelessness pressure, temporary accommodation, and blocked social lets rise, that supports the pressure-valve hypothesis.
- If local planning/HMO conversion rises in the same areas as specified accommodation claims, that supports the property conversion hypothesis.
- If FOI or local records show large out-of-area referral shares, that supports the import/dumping hypothesis.
- If asylum support/refugee move-on indicators rise locally, that supports one migration-related pathway, but not all migration.
- If prison/probation release indicators align with referral evidence, that supports the criminal justice pathway.

But without resident-level or placement-level lineage, much of the national dashboard will remain circumstantial.

## What we can probably answer

With public data and moderate scraping:

- Which areas have rising homelessness and temporary accommodation pressure?
- Which areas have falling or insufficient social housing move-on capacity?
- Which areas have rising ordinary HMO planning activity?
- Which areas have rising supported/exempt housing planning activity?
- Which HMO applications are likely ordinary shared housing rather than supported/exempt accommodation?
- Which applicants/agents/providers appear repeatedly?
- Which areas have high or rising Housing Benefit specified accommodation counts, at least from the improved 2025/2026 data onward?
- Which areas have asylum support/resettlement populations that may create additional local pressure?
- Which areas have broader population or age-structure changes?
- Which areas show social housing stock loss through Right to Buy or other sales?
- Which boroughs are likely candidates for deeper FOI work?

That is enough for a pressure and anomaly dashboard.

## What we cannot yet answer cleanly

Without FOI, local administrative data, or provider data:

- What percentage of supported/exempt residents in a borough were born locally?
- What percentage had a local connection?
- What percentage came from another borough?
- Which borough or institution referred them?
- How many came from prison, hospital, asylum/refugee move-on, care leaving, domestic abuse, mental health pathways, or ordinary homelessness?
- How long did they stay?
- Where did they go next?
- How often did they cycle back?
- Which providers produce good outcomes?
- Which providers mainly churn residents?
- How much of the increase is true need versus reclassification of housing stock?
- How much is imported demand versus local demand?

Those are the questions that matter most. They are also the least available in clean public data.

## Best current answer to "where did the extra people come from?"

The best current answer is not "one group".

It is:

1. Some people are local residents who would previously have moved into social housing, ordinary private rented rooms, hostels, or other lower-support pathways, but those exits became blocked or unaffordable.
2. Some people are already in homelessness or temporary accommodation systems and are moved into supported/exempt accommodation because it is available and may be cheaper for the local authority than hotels or nightly-paid TA.
3. Some people are discharged or released from institutions: prisons, hospitals, care systems, and possibly asylum accommodation after status decisions.
4. Some people come from outside the borough because providers accept out-of-area referrals and other institutions learn that the borough has rooms.
5. Some demand is created by the category itself: once the room exists and pays, the system finds people who can occupy it.

That last sentence is important and uncomfortable. It does not mean the people are fake. It means a public payment category can create a search process for eligible humans.

The person is real. The vulnerability may be real. The housing need may be real. But the scale and geography of the category can still be shaped by the revenue model.

## Why this is not just "fraud"

There may be fraud. Birmingham's strategy notes evidence of fraud in parts of the sector, including ghost tenants and fraudulent claims. But a pure fraud model is probably not the main explanation.

The more important model is legal or semi-legal extraction from a badly designed payment and placement system:

- real people;
- real rooms;
- real homelessness or discharge pressure;
- real Housing Benefit claims;
- weak support;
- weak move-on;
- weak enforcement;
- high rents;
- fragmented accountability.

This is why the problem is more serious than "some empty HMOs claiming money". Empty-room fraud is a policing problem. A functioning market that profitably warehouses vulnerable people is a system-design problem.

## One-sentence version

The incentive is obvious; the hard part is proving how a changing pool of real people and a changing stock of rooms were routed into a payment category whose profitability may itself have helped create, import, retain, and reclassify the demand it appears to serve.
