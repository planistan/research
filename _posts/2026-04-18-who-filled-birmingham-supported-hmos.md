Between 2018 and 2021, the number of "transitional" supported housing units in Birmingham almost doubled. By mid-2021, the city had reached a staggering scale: **21,317 units** of supported accommodation (Source: SH-001, p.5).

This growth creates a major structural puzzle. In the city’s own *Supported Housing Strategy 2022-2025*, the estimated total local need for this type of housing was placed between **8,505 and 10,455 units** (p.17).

Even using the most generous estimate of need, Birmingham had over **10,800 rooms** more than its own population required. This was not a minor oversupply; it was a city-scale surplus. The obvious question is: who was filling those rooms?

### Not a Local Explosion

To understand the gap, we must first discard the idea that Birmingham suffered a sudden, organic explosion in local support needs. There is no evidence that the number of care leavers, domestic abuse survivors, or people with mental health issues in Birmingham doubled in three years.

Instead, the evidence suggests that the growth was **supply-led**. The "exempt" accommodation model—which allows providers to bypass standard Local Housing Allowance caps—made low-support HMO rooms financially attractive. Once the supply existed, it didn't stay empty. Real people filled those rooms, but they didn't necessarily come from Birmingham's own housing list.

### Three Mechanisms of Occupancy

The surplus rooms were filled through three primary mechanisms: institutional routing, blocked exits, and the stock-flow trap.

#### 1. Institutional Routing (Institutional Dumping)
When local authorities, prisons, and hospitals elsewhere in the country cannot find local housing, they look for where supply exists. Birmingham, with its massive stock of non-commissioned HMOs, became the default national sink.

The research by University of Birmingham/Spring Housing (SH-004) provides the smoking gun for this routing. They identified 129 separate referral or access routes into Birmingham's exempt sector. Excluding internet searches and word-of-mouth, **46 of those 129 referral points (35%) were situated outside of Birmingham** (p.25).

This routing is heavily driven by the criminal justice system. The report identifies direct referrals from **fourteen different prisons**, many of which were outside the West Midlands estate. One criminal justice professional quoted in the report noted:

> "There is a significant issue with people being released into Birmingham and I think it is because Birmingham has the housing stock and has developed the market... many out of area prisons declaring to them that the only option is Birmingham." (SH-004, p.27)

Crucially, the providers themselves facilitate this. Unlike social housing, which usually requires a "local connection" to the borough, **none of the non-commissioned providers interviewed had a local connection policy** (p.26). One provider admitted that their referrals were "predominantly from out of area sources," rationalizing it as "how it’s ended up" rather than a deliberate strategy.

#### 2. Blocked Exits: The Stock-Flow Trap
A housing system is a flow, not a static count. If people enter a system but cannot leave, the total "stock" of occupied rooms will rise even if the inflow remains constant.

In Birmingham, the "move-on" routes are effectively blocked. Private rents are unaffordable for those on benefits, and social housing queues are decades long. This creates a **stock-flow trap**: people are moved into a "transitional" HMO intended for a few months of stay, but they find themselves stuck for years.

The city’s data shows that while 44% of occupants had lived in their property for less than 12 months, **12% had been there for over 3 years** (SH-001, p.8). In a sector designed for short-term transition, a 12% "stuck" rate across 21,000 units represents thousands of rooms that never become available for new local cases.

#### 3. Reclassification of Ordinary Need
The final mechanism is the reclassification of ordinary low-income housing need as "supported" need. Because ordinary HMOs are often unaffordable or unavailable to people on benefits, individuals who simply need a cheap room are routed into the "exempt" sector. They are given "minimal" support—often less than two hours a week (SH-001, p.19)—solely to justify the higher rent claim.

### What Is Not Yet Settled

While the routing machinery is now well-documented, the exact demographic composition of the marginal residents remains unquantified. We know that **institutional routing** is happening, but we do not yet have a settled percentage for:

- **Migration share:** What proportion of the 21,000 residents are recent international migrants, refugees leaving asylum accommodation, or UK internal migrants?
- **Local connection rates:** Beyond the referral *points*, what is the actual percentage of the *total resident population* that lacks a local connection to Birmingham?

The 2024 Birmingham Homelessness Strategy notes that **47% of households in temporary accommodation** migrated into the city, but this figure describes the statutory TA system, not necessarily the wider 21,000-unit exempt HMO market.

### How to Settle the Argument

To move beyond speculative claims, we need a transparent, anonymized breakdown of the resident population. A simplified version of the "data wishlist" required to settle this would look like this:

| The Question | Required Data |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Are they local?** | Prior postcode/Local Authority of all residents at point of entry. |
| **Who sent them?** | Referral source (Out-of-area Council, Prison, Probation, Hospital, or Self-referral). |
| **Are they stuck?** | Average length of stay vs. move-on destination. |
| **Are they support cases?** | Audited support hours delivered vs. assessed support need. |

Until this data is published, Birmingham remains a city serving a national housing crisis with a local planning and oversight system that was never designed to handle it. The 10,000-room gap is not just a statistical oddity; it is evidence of a massive, state-funded routing system that has detached housing supply from local need.


### Blocked Exits: Why Move-On Fails

The biggest missing piece in most debates is **exit capacity**. Even if referrals stopped growing, this stock could stay high if people cannot move on.

For many residents, the realistic exits are limited:

- social housing supply is structurally short and waiting times are long;
- private rents are above what low-income single adults can sustain;
- LHA and real-market rent gaps make ordinary private rooms hard to access;
- landlords often avoid higher-risk or benefit-dependent tenants;
- there is very little cheap, single-room move-on stock.

This is why "transitional" can become semi-permanent. People are placed into a supported room because it is the only immediate option, then remain because no viable next step appears. That is not a claim about individual motivation. It is a system-design result when inflow pathways are faster than outflow pathways.

### The Stock-Flow Trap

This can be expressed as a simple stock-flow problem.

- **Inflow:** people referred from homelessness, criminal justice, care-leaver, health, and other crisis routes.
- **Outflow:** people moving into ordinary private renting, social housing, family re-accommodation, or other settled exits.

If outflow slows below inflow, total occupied rooms rise even without a dramatic jump in new referrals. In other words, occupancy can accumulate through duration, not just through new entries.

The available Birmingham evidence is consistent with this trap. SH-001 reports that 44% of occupants had been in place for less than 12 months, but 12% had been there for more than 3 years (p.8). In a sector described as transitional, a long-stay tail of that size can absorb a large number of rooms over time.

So the growth pattern does not require a single explanatory shock. It can emerge from three interacting conditions:

1. out-of-area routing into available stock;
2. weak local-connection gatekeeping in non-commissioned provision;
3. blocked exits from supported rooms into ordinary housing.

### What Would Settle This (Minimal Evidence Table)

This is a research gap, not a rhetorical gap. The way to settle the argument is a small, repeatable evidence table at resident-entry and move-on points.

At minimum, for each year and provider type:

- **Referral source:** council, prison/probation, hospital/discharge, care-leaver team, self/provider route.
- **Previous location:** prior local authority and anonymized prior postcode band.
- **Local connection status:** whether the person had a Birmingham local connection at placement.
- **Support intensity:** assessed need and support hours delivered (auditable, not brochure claims).
- **Move-on destination:** private rent, social housing, another supported unit, temporary accommodation, custody/hospital, unknown.

With those fields, key disputes become measurable:

- Is growth mainly local demand or external routing?
- Are people moving through, or being parked in place?
- Is support intensity matched to classification and rent level?

Without those fields, the public can see stock growth and referral-route signals, but not the full resident-level mechanism that explains who filled the 10,000-room gap.
