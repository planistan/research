### The Economic Threshold of the \"Tiny Room\" Model

In our research into Greenwich, we've identified the \"LHA-Targeted\" HMO: 6.51m² rooms designed to capture the maximum Local Housing Allowance subsidy. But every investment has a tipping point. This case study explores the financial boundary where the HMO model fails and \"reversion\" to a family home (C3) becomes the rational move.


#### 1. The Cost of Conversion (The Entry Price)
Converting a 3-bed Victorian terrace into a 6-bed HMO isn't just about putting locks on doors.
- **Fire Safety:** FD30 doors, integrated alarm systems, emergency lighting.
- **Plumbing:** Squeezing in en-suites or additional shower rooms.
- **Partitions:** Stud walls to subdivide larger rooms.
- **Soundproofing:** Required by building regs but often the first thing skimped on.
- **Licensing Fees:** Mandatory for large HMOs.

*Estimated cost: £30k - £60k per property.*

#### 2. The Sunk Cost Trap
Once a house is carved up, it becomes a specialized asset. Converting it back to a "normal" family home involves:
- Stripping out excess plumbing and en-suites (often in awkward places).
- Removing partition walls and repairing ceilings/floors.
- Full redecoration to remove the "bedsitting room" aesthetic.
- Losing the income stream for 3-6 months during works.

#### 3. The Yield vs. Capital Growth Tipping Point
If house prices in a borough like Greenwich rise by 20%, does the landlord sell? 
- **The HMO Yield:** Stable, benefit-backed income (e.g., £45k/year gross).
- **The C3 Value:** A single family home might sell for £550k, but as an HMO, its "bricks and mortar" value is often *lower* to an owner-occupier because of the conversion damage.

**The Question:** At what price point does the capital gain from a "clean" family home sale outweigh the 10-12% levered yield of a cramped HMO?

#### 4. The Exit Strategy
Most LHA-targeted landlords are yield-seekers, not capital-growth seekers. They are often "locked in" by the debt used to finance the conversion. We need to model the **Exit Price**: the point where the cost of reversion + the lost rent + the CGT = more than the value of the ongoing benefit-backed cash flow.
