Planning applications are statutory public records. UK local authorities are legally required to publish them so that neighbours, journalists, researchers, and the public can scrutinise proposed developments. That transparency is the entire point of the planning system.

## Aggregation serves, not subverts, that purpose

Planistan collects and aggregates planning application data from London borough portals. We believe this is not only lawful but actively consistent with the intent of data protection law. Under UK GDPR Article 6, processing is lawful where it serves a legitimate interest — and the ICO's own guidance identifies transparency and accountability as legitimate interests, particularly where disclosure is in the interest of the general public rather than the controller.

The theory of [contextual integrity](https://nissenbaum.tech.cornell.edu/papers/H.%20Nissenbaum,%20_Privacy%20as%20Contextual%20Integrity.pdf) (Nissenbaum, 2004) holds that information flows are appropriate when they match the norms of the original context. Planning applications are published by law, in a public register, explicitly for public scrutiny. Aggregating them into a searchable database is consistent with that context — it makes the intended transparency more effective, not different in kind.

## The commercial actor distinction

Applicants, agents, and case officers named in planning applications are acting in a professional or commercial capacity on public infrastructure. They are not private individuals going about their personal lives. A property developer submitting hundreds of applications, or an architecture firm acting as agent across dozens of schemes, is operating at scale on the built environment of a city. The public interest in being able to see and analyse those patterns is substantial.

GDPR was designed to protect individuals from large-scale actors — corporations, governments, and institutions that accumulate and exploit personal data. Applying it to prevent public scrutiny of large-scale commercial actors is an inversion of that purpose. The regulation is not intended to make public records harder to use; it is intended to stop powerful institutions from using personal data against the people those institutions serve.

If a case officer is being threatened by a developer and their name needs protection, that is a serious matter — but the answer is to say so explicitly and address it, not to quietly remove names from statutory public records and pretend the problem does not exist.

## What we store

We store names as they appear in statutory public planning records: applicant names, agent names, and case officer names. We do not store addresses, telephone numbers, or any data beyond what the council has published in the planning register.

If you are a private individual and your name appears in our data in a context you believe is genuinely disproportionate — for example, a single personal home extension — you are welcome to contact us at [planistan@proton.me](mailto:planistan@proton.me) and we will consider your request on its merits.
