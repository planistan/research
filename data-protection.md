Planning applications are statutory public records. UK local authorities are legally required to publish them so that neighbours, journalists, researchers, and the public can scrutinise proposed developments. That transparency is the entire point of the planning system.

## Aggregation serves, not subverts, that purpose

Planistan collects and aggregates planning application data from London borough portals. We believe this is not only lawful but actively consistent with the intent of data protection law. Under UK GDPR Article 6, processing is lawful where it serves a legitimate interest — and the ICO's own guidance identifies transparency and accountability as legitimate interests, particularly where disclosure is in the interest of the general public rather than the controller.

The theory of [contextual integrity](https://nissenbaum.tech.cornell.edu/papers/H.%20Nissenbaum,%20_Privacy%20as%20Contextual%20Integrity.pdf) (Nissenbaum, 2004) holds that information flows are appropriate when they match the norms of the original context. Planning applications are published by law, in a public register, explicitly for public scrutiny. Aggregating them into a searchable database is consistent with that context — it makes the intended transparency more effective, not different in kind.

## Who GDPR protects — and who it does not

GDPR protects natural persons. It explicitly does not apply to legal entities — companies, partnerships, LLPs, or any other corporate form (Recital 14). A substantial proportion of the actors in planning data are legal entities: developer companies, architecture firms, planning consultancies. GDPR affords them no protection at all.

For natural persons, GDPR protections are at their weakest when those persons are acting in a professional or commercial capacity on statutory public records. A sole trader architect or individual developer submitting planning applications is doing so in their professional capacity, on public infrastructure, in a public register maintained by law. The legitimate interest basis for presenting that data — in the interest of public transparency and accountability — is strong.

GDPR was designed to protect natural persons in their private lives from powerful institutions that accumulate and exploit personal data. Applying it to prevent public scrutiny of professionals and companies operating on public infrastructure is an inversion of that purpose.

If a case officer is being threatened by a developer and their name needs protection, that is a serious matter — but the answer is to say so explicitly and address it, not to quietly remove fields from statutory public records and pretend the problem does not exist.

## What we present

Planistan presents fields as they appear in statutory public planning records. We do not store data beyond what the council has published in the planning register, and we present it in the same spirit of transparency the register exists to serve.

If you are a natural person and a field in our data relates to you in a context you believe is genuinely disproportionate — for example, a single personal home extension — you are welcome to contact us at [planistan@proton.me](mailto:planistan@proton.me) and we will consider your request on its merits.
