# Structural demand: where did the extra people come from?

Status: private research note
Created: 2026-04-18

## Question

Supported/exempt HMOs only work if rooms are occupied by real people. If the boom was not mainly fake empty-room fraud, then where did the extra people come from?

## Assumptions to challenge

### A. "Social housing stock is not being destroyed at a meaningful rate"

This is probably wrong in the wider housing-system sense.

The homes are not all physically destroyed, but social-rent availability can be destroyed by:

- Right to Buy and non-replacement;
- stock transfer into housing association / non-council systems;
- demolition/regeneration gaps;
- decanting and unusable stock;
- stock failing decent-homes standards;
- fewer lettings because tenants cannot move on;
- affordable/social rent being replaced by private or "affordable rent" products that do not serve the same people.

For the supported-HMO question, what matters is not total dwellings. It is the supply of **cheap, accessible, single-adult move-on accommodation**. That can collapse even if the headline dwelling count does not.

### B. "Natural population is not growing because TFR is low"

This is too simple. TFR can be low while a city still has natural increase if it has a young age structure.

Birmingham's own 2022 population estimate table shows births exceeded deaths every year from 2012 to 2021. Natural increase fell over the decade but was still positive. The same table shows large internal out-migration and significant international in-migration, with international net migration especially large in 2021.

For supported HMOs, total population growth still may not be the main driver. The relevant pool is not "all residents"; it is single adults and households who cannot access ordinary housing.

### C. "HMOs need real humans"

Yes. The working assumption should be real occupancy, not completely empty fake HMOs.

There can still be fraud or abuse around support provision, rent levels, service charges, property condition, referral quality, and eligibility thresholds. But the basic model appears to require real occupants in rooms.

### D. "The council has an incentive because real people would otherwise be in hotels/temporary accommodation"

Partly yes, but distinguish two streams:

- families in temporary accommodation / B&Bs / hotels;
- single adults in supported/exempt HMOs.

Those streams overlap politically and financially but are not identical. Birmingham's 2024 temporary-accommodation pressure mostly appears family-heavy, while the classic supported/exempt HMO boom is more single-adult/transitional supported accommodation.

## Better explanation

The extra occupied supported-HMO rooms can be explained by three things moving together:

### 1. More people in the relevant vulnerable single-adult pool

Possible inflows:

- asylum/refugee move-on and other migrant routes;
- prison release and probation homelessness;
- care leavers;
- domestic abuse and household breakdown;
- mental health/substance misuse;
- ordinary low-income single adults losing private tenancies;
- people excluded from family/sofa-surfing arrangements.

Birmingham's homelessness strategy says 30% of households presenting as statutorily homeless had come into the UK through a migrant route, and this rises to 47% for households in temporary accommodation. That is a major signal, though it is homelessness/TA data rather than direct supported-HMO resident-origin data.

### 2. Ordinary housing exits being blocked

Even with the same inflow, the stock of people in supported HMOs rises if move-on slows.

Blocked exits include:

- too little social housing;
- unaffordable private rents;
- LHA/private-rent gaps;
- landlords unwilling to take benefit claimants or high-risk tenants;
- no single-room move-on supply;
- low incomes, debts, arrears, deposits, guarantor barriers.

This creates a stock-flow trap: people enter supported/exempt accommodation but cannot leave, so occupied rooms accumulate.

### 3. Other systems dumping people into Birmingham

The University of Birmingham / Spring Housing report gives direct evidence of this mechanism:

- criminal justice organisations and local authority departments were the largest referral clusters;
- in both clusters, most referring organisations were outside Birmingham;
- 46 of 129 identified referral points were outside Birmingham, excluding internet and word-of-mouth routes;
- interviewed providers did not have strict Birmingham local-connection policies;
- criminal justice interview evidence said out-of-area prisons treated Birmingham as the only option because it had housing stock and an established market.

That does not answer birthplace/nationality. It answers institutional routing: agencies outside Birmingham had people to place and used Birmingham.

## Current brutal answer

The supported-HMO boom probably does require structural change in the relevant population, but not just in the naive "Birmingham had more births" sense.

The structural change is:

> more people unable to access ordinary housing, plus fewer exits into social/private move-on housing, plus a state-funded HMO category that made it profitable to collect and hold those people in Birmingham.

Migration/asylum/refugee routes are likely a major part of the homelessness pressure and should be quantified directly. But they are not the only mechanism. Prison/probation, care leavers, household breakdown, private-rent failure, and social-housing shortage can all create real people who fill rooms.

## What would settle the argument

For supported/exempt residents, by year:

| Question | Required measure |
| --- | --- |
| Are they local? | previous postcode/LA and Birmingham local-connection status |
| Are they migrants? | country of birth, nationality, years in UK, asylum/refugee route if lawfully held |
| Who sent them? | referral source: council, prison, probation, hospital, self, provider, charity |
| What changed? | previous accommodation and date of move-in |
| Are they stuck? | length of stay and move-on destination |
| Are they really support cases? | assessed support need and support hours delivered |
| Is it profitable? | HB rent, service charges, lease cost, support cost |

Until that table exists, the safest claim is:

> Birmingham's supported/exempt HMO boom was filled by real people, but the public evidence currently identifies the routing machinery more clearly than the demographic composition. Migration-route homelessness is clearly material in Birmingham; out-of-area institutional dumping is also evidenced; the exact split needs data.
