# Draft post note: the cobra effect in supported housing

Status: rough private post seed
Created: 2026-04-18

## Working title

The cobra effect in supported housing

## Core idea

The supported-exempt accommodation boom may be a cobra effect.

The state wanted to fund accommodation for people with support needs. It created a payment structure where supported/exempt accommodation could receive higher Housing Benefit than ordinary private renting. The market then had a reason to produce more claimable supported-housing units.

That does not mean residents are fake. The cobras were real too. The point is that the state may have created a category that is profitable to expand, and real people can be imported, retained, or reclassified into that category.

## Simple version

1. Ordinary HMO room: rent is constrained.
2. Supported-exempt HMO room: higher rent can be claimed through Housing Benefit.
3. Provider/landlord discovers the yield.
4. More houses are leased or converted.
5. People who need somewhere to go are routed into those rooms.
6. Some people may have genuine support needs; others may mainly have housing need plus thin support.
7. Every occupied room becomes a public-money revenue stream.

The market does not need to create humans from nothing. Mass migration, asylum move-on, homelessness, prison release, household breakdown, and the lack of proper social housing can create a real pool of people needing somewhere to go. The market only needs to find, import, retain, or reclassify enough people from that pool to keep the rooms claimable.

## Why Birmingham matters

Birmingham appears to have had the conditions for the cobra effect to scale:

- convertible housing stock;
- high numbers of non-commissioned units;
- weak local control over supply;
- providers without strict local-connection rules;
- other councils/agencies/prisons with people they could not house locally;
- Housing Benefit money available once a room qualified.

## Origin of the analogy

The cobra story is usually said to have happened in Delhi under the British Raj: colonial authorities supposedly paid a bounty for dead cobras, people bred cobras for the reward, and the problem worsened when the bounty ended. The term "cobra effect" was popularised by economist Horst Siebert, but the historical basis of the Delhi cobra story is disputed.

The more solid historical parallel is the 1902 Hanoi rat-tail bounty under French Indochina. French authorities paid for rat tails during a rat-control campaign. The incentive reportedly led to rats being released without tails and to rat-farming around the city.

Use the Hanoi version when the post needs a historically grounded example:

- Place: Hanoi, Tonkin, French Indochina, now Vietnam.
- Date: 1902.
- Policy: French colonial authorities paid a bounty for rat tails during a plague/public-health rat-control campaign.
- Failure mode: paying for tails rather than solved infestation rewarded tail production. Reports included rats released without tails and alleged rat farms around Hanoi.
- Lesson: the animals were real; the incentive made the countable bounty object more valuable than actually eliminating the problem.

## Institutional memory

Do not frame this as "India remembers the payday". That turns a policy mechanism into a lazy cultural claim.

The useful question is institutional memory inside modern housing/payment systems. Markets remember where the money is. Once a category becomes claimable, the knowledge spreads through:

- landlords and property sourcers who learn which leases produce the best yield;
- managing agents and consultants who package the model for others;
- registered providers willing to front or lease schemes;
- support providers who learn how little support can still sustain a claim;
- councils and referral agencies that learn which providers will accept hard-to-place people;
- prisons, probation teams, hospitals, care-leaver teams, and homelessness services that learn Birmingham has rooms;
- investors who learn that public Housing Benefit can underwrite cash flow.

This is the modern institutional memory of the payday. It is not a single mastermind and it is not cultural inheritance from the original cobra/rabbit/rat story. It is operational learning inside a subsidy market.

Possible line:

> The institutional memory is not in Delhi or Hanoi; it is in the market. Providers learn the claimable category, referrers learn where beds are, and investors learn where public money can be turned into rent.

## Possible thesis

> Birmingham did not just discover more vulnerability. It became a place where real vulnerability, migration pressure, homelessness, prison release, and social-housing failure could be monetised. The public payment system rewarded claimable supported-housing rooms, and the market responded by producing them.
