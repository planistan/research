# Demand-side investigation: who filled supported-exempt HMOs?

Status: private research draft
Created: 2026-04-18

## Corrected framing

The question is not "why is supported housing demand up?" in the abstract. That framing hides the point.

The sharper question is:

> Who are the marginal residents who filled the supported-exempt HMO boom, and what changed in the resident pipeline compared with ordinary HMO demand?

Avoid euphemisms. "Out-of-area referral pipeline" means this:

> Councils, prisons, probation teams, homelessness services, care-leaver teams, hospitals, providers, or informal referral routes outside Birmingham sent people to Birmingham because Birmingham had rooms they could use. In plainer terms: some institutions appear to have dumped people they could not or would not house locally into Birmingham's supported-exempt accommodation market.

That is not yet a quantified claim about birthplace, ethnicity, nationality, or migration status. It is a claim about **institutional routing and prior location**.

There are two different forms of demand:

- resident need for accommodation/support;
- provider/investor demand for claimable supported-exempt units.

The money still requires people in rooms. So the key issue is where those people came from and why they were routed into supported-exempt HMOs rather than ordinary HMOs, social housing, temporary accommodation, hostels, asylum accommodation, prison resettlement accommodation, or family/private renting.

## Working hypothesis

Birmingham's boom was probably not caused by a sudden local explosion in domestic abuse, mental illness, or organic support need.

The more plausible mechanism is a pipeline shift:

1. housing shortage and welfare rules made ordinary single-adult accommodation hard to access;
2. exempt accommodation rules made low-support HMO rooms financially attractive to providers/landlords;
3. provider-led supply expanded quickly;
4. crisis systems with people to place used that supply;
5. Birmingham became a sink for people other places could not house.

This means the investigation should focus on **resident origin and referral source**, not just unit counts.

## Political economy framing

This does not need to be a simple conspiracy where one actor "controls Birmingham". It can emerge from incentives:

1. central government Housing Benefit rules make supported-exempt rent more valuable than ordinary HMO rent;
2. landlords, leaseholders, managing agents, registered providers, consultants, and support providers discover the yield;
3. more ordinary houses/HMOs are converted into claimable supported accommodation;
4. public agencies with people they cannot house locally use the new supply;
5. rent flows from the public purse to private or quasi-private actors;
6. the market becomes large enough that politicians, regulators, councils, landlords, and providers all face pressure not to break it abruptly.

This is profiteering from central-government funds even if no single person is forcing the whole system. It is a corrupting structure: weak rules create a revenue stream, private actors scale around it, residents become the claimable unit, and public agencies use the market because they have no better place to put people.

Be precise with language:

- **Profiteering** is a fair working hypothesis where high rents are paired with low support, weak oversight, and poor resident outcomes.
- **Institutional corruption** can mean a system has been bent toward private extraction from public funds.
- **Criminal corruption** or party-donation influence needs direct evidence before being stated as fact.
- In the UK context, say central-government or Housing Benefit funds, not federal funds.

## Cobra-effect framing

This may be a cobra-effect housing market.

The original "cobra effect" lesson is usually told as a bounty problem: pay people for dead cobras, and some people start breeding cobras. The policy pays for evidence that a problem has been handled, but the payment design rewards the production of more claimable problem-units.

Supported-exempt accommodation has a similar risk:

- problem: vulnerable people need housing and support;
- payment design: pay higher Housing Benefit for housing classed as supported/exempt;
- intended result: fund genuinely higher-cost supported housing;
- perverse incentive: create more claimable "supported" rooms, add low-cost support, and fill the rooms with people who can be made to qualify;
- system effect: more public money flows into the market, more people are routed into the category, and the underlying housing/support problem becomes harder to see.

Important correction: the cobras were real too. The point is not "fake demand". The point is that a payment system can reward the creation, importation, retention, or reclassification of real problem-units.

The cobra-effect claim is that the payment system can reward:

- reclassifying ordinary housing need as support need;
- importing people from other areas;
- lowering the threshold for "support";
- keeping people in place because occupied rooms are revenue;
- building a market that public agencies then depend on because other housing routes have failed.

Possible post line:

> Birmingham's supported-exempt accommodation boom looks like a cobra-effect housing market: the state paid extra for supported housing, and the market responded by producing more claimable supported-housing units.

Historical note:

- The "cobra effect" story is usually placed in Delhi under the British Raj, where colonial authorities supposedly paid a bounty for dead cobras and people bred cobras for the reward. The term was popularised by economist Horst Siebert, but the historical story appears shaky and may be apocryphal.
- The better-documented colonial bounty analogue is the 1902 Hanoi rat-tail bounty under French Indochina, where authorities paid for rat tails and the incentive encouraged tail-cutting, release, and alleged rat farming. In that version too, the animals were real. The problem was that the bounty rewarded producing/counting tails rather than reducing the rat problem.
- For this project the lesson is the incentive structure, not a claim about Indian cultural memory. The relevant institutional memory is in British/local-government/Housing Benefit/provider markets: actors learn that if a category is claimable, there is money in manufacturing capacity and routing people into that category.

Institutional memory here means operational learning inside the subsidy market:

- landlords learn which property forms produce higher rent;
- consultants and managing agents learn how to package the model;
- providers learn how to satisfy the support threshold;
- referral agencies learn which providers will take hard-to-place people;
- investors learn that Housing Benefit can underwrite revenue.

The payday does not need a conspiracy to persist. It persists because organisations learn the route from claimable category to cash flow.

## What changed?

Possible changes to test:

- More ordinary low-income single adults were reclassified as needing "support" because ordinary HMO/private rental routes were unaffordable or unavailable.
- More people were sent into Birmingham from outside the city because supply existed there.
- More prison/probation, homelessness, care-leaver, mental-health, and hospital-discharge pathways used supported HMOs as quick placement options.
- Asylum/refugee move-on and migrant homelessness may have increased flows into homelessness and supported housing, but should be measured rather than assumed.
- Low move-on supply increased duration of stay, so stock rose even if inflow did not rise as much.
- Providers may have actively sourced residents through self-referral, outreach, inter-provider transfers, and relationships with referring agencies.

## What exactly needs to be quantified

Do not hide behind "migration" as a catch-all. We need separate buckets:

- born/raised in Birmingham and still locally connected;
- UK-born but from another local authority;
- non-UK-born long-term Birmingham resident;
- recent international migrant outside the asylum system;
- asylum seeker or refused asylum seeker;
- refugee leaving asylum accommodation after a decision;
- prison leaver sent to Birmingham despite prior ties elsewhere;
- care leaver sent by another council;
- domestic abuse survivor moved for safety;
- person self-referring after hearing Birmingham providers had vacancies.

The politically charged question "what percentage are natively born in Birmingham?" is not identical to the operational question "who sent them here and why?" Both may matter, but the second is more likely to be answerable from housing/provider records.

## Migration question

Low TFR does not explain this housing stock change. The relevant flows are working-age adult flows:

- international migration;
- asylum and refugee move-on;
- internal migration;
- prison release/resettlement;
- homelessness displacement from other local authorities;
- household breakdown and eviction;
- movement from informal/family accommodation into claim-supported rooms.

Migration may be an important part of the story, but "migration did it" is too broad. The research needs to separate:

- newly arrived migrants;
- refugees leaving asylum accommodation after a decision;
- people with no recourse to public funds;
- UK internal migrants;
- deliberate placements into Birmingham by UK councils/agencies;
- Birmingham residents with no migration component.

If the data show that a large share came from asylum/refugee routes or recent international migration, say so plainly. If the data show that most came from UK criminal justice, homelessness, care-leaver, or inter-council dumping routes, say that plainly too.

## Birmingham facts to anchor

Birmingham's supported housing strategy reported an estimated supported-housing need of roughly 8,505 to 10,455 units, against about 21,317 units of supported accommodation in scope in June 2021.

That gap is the central fact. It implies the city had much more supported accommodation than its own estimated local need. The explanation must therefore include some combination of:

- residents arriving from outside Birmingham;
- ordinary housing need being routed into supported accommodation;
- weak needs assessment / low support thresholds;
- longer stays due to lack of move-on;
- provider-led supply creating its own referral channels.

The follow-up question is just as important:

> Why did the sending areas have people to dump into Birmingham in the first place?

Possible answers to test:

- they lacked cheap single-room accommodation locally;
- local Housing Benefit/LHA rates and private rents made ordinary HMO/private renting impossible;
- social housing queues and temporary accommodation were full;
- prison/probation release duties created immediate accommodation pressure;
- asylum/refugee move-on created sudden homelessness duties;
- care leaver and adult social care teams used Birmingham because it was cheaper or faster than local provision;
- Birmingham providers accepted people with fewer questions than local commissioned services;
- sending agencies faced budget or legal pressure to place someone anywhere that would take them.

## Groups to quantify

The post should try to split the marginal resident population into:

- Birmingham local-connection homeless people;
- people placed or dumped by other local authorities/agencies;
- prison/probation referrals;
- care leavers and young people;
- domestic abuse survivors;
- mental health/substance misuse;
- refugees/asylum move-on/new migrants;
- ordinary low-income single adults reclassified as needing support;
- inter-provider transfers;
- self-referrals generated by the existence of supply.

## Draft post angle

Working title:

**Who filled Birmingham's supported-exempt HMOs?**

Thesis to test:

> The supported-exempt accommodation boom looks less like a simple rise in organic local support need and more like a dumping/routing system for single-adult housing need. Birmingham's cheap HMO stock, high exempt-rent yield, weak gatekeeping, and available providers made it possible for other systems to send people to Birmingham or reclassify ordinary housing need as supported-housing need.

## Claims not yet safe

- Do not claim migration is the main driver until resident-origin data supports it.
- Do not hide institutional dumping behind phrases like "referral pipeline". If agencies outside Birmingham sent people there, say that plainly and quantify it.
- Do not claim domestic abuse increased; treat it as a referral category, not a causal explanation.
- Do not claim rooms were left empty; the model generally needs occupied rooms.
- Do not equate all supported-exempt residents with high support needs; low-support cases are exactly what needs measuring.
