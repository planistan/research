# Evidence extracts: resident pipeline

Status: working extracts from fetched sources
Created: 2026-04-18

## SH-001 Birmingham supported housing strategy

Early findings:

- Provider survey evidence says most supported housing provision was in converted houses.
- Most properties were leased by providers, and most lease arrangements were short term.
- Provider survey occupancy data showed many occupants had lived in their property for less than 12 months.
- Birmingham estimated non-commissioned transitional supported housing at around 20,000 units, about 94% of the sector.
- Local Authority or statutory commissioning oversight covered only a small estimated share of provision.
- The strategy supports the idea that the market was dominated by provider-led, short-lease, converted-house provision rather than planned council-commissioned need.

Use in post:

- This supports the supply/referral-machine hypothesis.
- It does not by itself identify resident origin.

## SH-004 University of Birmingham / Spring Housing report

Strong resident-origin evidence:

- Criminal justice organisations and local authority departments were the two largest referral clusters identified.
- In both clusters, most referring organisations were outside Birmingham.
- The local authority referral category included care-leaver teams and housing-options departments from the wider West Midlands, plus two from South East England.
- The criminal justice category included direct referrals from fourteen prisons, several outside the West Midlands and Staffordshire prison estate.
- Excluding internet property sites and word of mouth, 46 of 129 identified referral points were outside Birmingham.
- None of the interviewed accommodation providers had a strict Birmingham local-connection policy.
- One provider said their referrals were predominantly from out-of-area sources.
- A criminal-justice professional reportedly described Birmingham as attractive because it had housing stock and an established market; out-of-area prisons treated Birmingham as the only option.

Use in post:

- This is the strongest current evidence that Birmingham's resident base was not just local organic need.
- It supports a mixed mechanism: other councils/agencies/prisons sending people to Birmingham, prison/probation, wider local-authority housing/care-leaver pathways, and low-conditionality access.
- Plain language: some institutions outside Birmingham appear to have dumped people into Birmingham because Birmingham had rooms and fewer barriers.
- It does not prove migrants/refugees are the dominant marginal group.

## SH-003 NAO supported housing investigation

Early findings:

- NAO says there are no good data on the numbers of units and people living in supported housing.
- NAO identifies exempt accommodation as outside normal local Housing Benefit caps, enabling higher rents.
- NAO frames the sector as fragmented across DWP, DLUHC, DHSC, local authorities, and other departments.

Use in post:

- This justifies why the post needs a data-discovery section rather than pretending the answer is already in a clean public dataset.

## Current answer to "what changed?"

Evidence now supports this as the first serious formulation:

> Birmingham did not simply experience a sudden increase in local support need. The exempt-accommodation market expanded supply in converted, leased houses, and criminal justice, councils, homelessness services and other crisis systems used Birmingham because it had available stock and low-conditionality access. Some institutions outside Birmingham appear to have dumped people into the city, and some ordinary or crisis single-adult housing need appears to have been routed into "supported" accommodation.

Still unresolved:

- share of residents who were migrants/refugees/asylum move-on;
- share who were Birmingham-born or long-term Birmingham residents;
- share who were UK-born but sent from another council/agency;
- share with Birmingham local connection;
- share whose support need was minimal or primarily housing need;
- annual change in referral sources;
- whether increased stock was driven more by inflow, longer stays, or both.
